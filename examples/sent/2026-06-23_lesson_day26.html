Day 26: Transfer Learning Fundamentals
TL;DR
Transfer learning allows AI models to reuse features learned from one task and apply them to another, similar task, which can greatly speed up the training process and improve model performance.

In plain English

Transfer learning is a technique used in machine learning where a model trained on one task is re-purposed or fine-tuned for another related task. This is useful because training a model from scratch can be computationally expensive and require large amounts of data.
The idea behind transfer learning is that the features learned from the first task are general enough to be useful for the second task, even if the tasks are not identical. For example, a model trained to recognize objects in images can be fine-tuned to recognize specific types of objects, such as animals or vehicles.
However, transfer learning is not always successful, and it can fail if the tasks are too dissimilar or if the data distributions are too different. In such cases, the model may need to be trained from scratch or additional training data may be required.

Think of it this way

Transfer learning is like moving to a new city and finding that your existing map skills are still useful, even if the city is different. You already know how to read maps, understand street layouts, and navigate, so you can quickly adapt to the new city without having to learn everything from scratch.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

Transfer learning is a crucial technique in AI engineering and ML engineering roles, as it allows for the efficient deployment of models in production environments. It is closely related to concepts such as fine-tuning, model evaluation, and observability, and is often mentioned in job descriptions as a desirable skill, particularly in relation to RAG (Retrieve, Augment, Generate) agents and Model Serving Platforms.
One thing to remember
When applying transfer learning, it's essential to carefully evaluate the similarity between the original task and the target task to determine whether transfer learning is likely to be successful.
