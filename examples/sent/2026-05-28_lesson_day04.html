Day 4: Understanding Bias vs Variance Tradeoff
TL;DR
The bias-variance tradeoff is a fundamental concept in machine learning that involves finding a balance between underfitting and overfitting to achieve optimal model performance.

In plain English

In machine learning, models can suffer from two main issues: underfitting and overfitting. Underfitting occurs when a model is too simple and fails to capture the underlying patterns in the data, resulting in poor performance on both training and testing sets.
On the other hand, overfitting happens when a model is too complex and learns the noise in the training data, performing well on the training set but poorly on the testing set.
A learning curve is a graphical representation of a model's performance on the training and testing sets as the size of the training set increases, which can help diagnose underfitting or overfitting.

Think of it this way

Think of underfitting and overfitting like cooking a meal. If you undercook your meal, it will be too raw and inedible, just like an underfitting model that fails to capture the patterns in the data. On the other hand, if you overcook your meal, it will be too burnt and inedible, similar to an overfitting model that learns the noise in the training data.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

Diagnosing model failures due to underfitting or overfitting is crucial in AI engineering roles, as it directly impacts the model's performance in production. For instance, a model that is overfitting may require regularization techniques or early stopping to prevent overfitting, while an underfitting model may need more complex features or a different model architecture. This skill is often required in job descriptions that mention model evaluation, fine-tuning, and production concerns.
One thing to remember
A model's performance on the training set is not always a good indicator of its performance on the testing set, and a learning curve can be a valuable tool in diagnosing underfitting or overfitting.
