Day 10: Mastering Decision Trees and Random Forests for AI Engineering Roles
TL;DR
Decision Trees and Random Forests are powerful machine learning algorithms used for classification and regression tasks, particularly effective in handling tabular data.

In plain English

Decision Trees are a type of machine learning model that work by splitting data into subsets based on features. This process of splitting continues until a stopping criterion is met, such as when all instances in a node belong to the same class. The tree is grown by recursively partitioning the data into smaller subsets.
Random Forests are an ensemble method that combines multiple Decision Trees to improve the accuracy and robustness of predictions. By aggregating the predictions of multiple trees, Random Forests can reduce overfitting and improve generalization to unseen data.
Feature importance is a key aspect of Random Forests, as it allows us to understand which features are driving the predictions. This can be useful for interpreting the results and identifying the most relevant features for the problem at hand.

Think of it this way

The process of growing a Decision Tree can be thought of like planning a road trip. You start at a central location and make a series of decisions about which road to take, based on the features of the road, such as traffic, distance, and road conditions. As you make each decision, you narrow down your options until you reach your final destination, just like a Decision Tree narrows down the data into smaller subsets until it makes a prediction.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

Mastering Decision Trees and Random Forests is crucial for AI Engineering roles, as they provide a strong baseline for tabular data and can often outperform Deep Learning models. This is particularly important for companies looking for solutions that are interpretable and can be deployed in production with minimal overhead, as signaled by job descriptions referencing RAG (Reasoning, Action, and Goals), agents, evaluations, fine-tuning, observability, and Model Serving Platforms (MCP).
One thing to remember
Random Forests are particularly effective because they combine the predictions of multiple Decision Trees, reducing overfitting and improving generalization to unseen data.
