Day 44: Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) Concepts for AI Engineers
TL;DR
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a two-stage approach that involves retrieving relevant information from a database and then using this information to guide the generation of text or other data.

In plain English

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique used in natural language processing and other areas of artificial intelligence. It works by first retrieving relevant information from a large database or knowledge base, and then using this information to generate new text, images, or other types of data. This approach is particularly useful when fine-tuning a model is not feasible due to the large size of the dataset or the complexity of the task.
The RAG approach has several advantages, including the ability to handle out-of-vocabulary words and to generate more accurate and informative text. It can also be used to improve the performance of language models on specific tasks, such as question answering or text summarization.
The key idea behind RAG is to use the retrieved information to augment the generation process, rather than relying solely on the model's internal knowledge or fine-tuning the model on a large dataset.

Think of it this way

The RAG approach can be thought of as a chef who is tasked with preparing a new dish. Instead of relying solely on their own knowledge and experience, the chef can first retrieve relevant recipes and cooking techniques from a cookbook or online database, and then use this information to guide the cooking process and generate a new and delicious dish.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

RAG matters in AI Engineering because it is a common approach used in many AI projects, including question answering systems, text summarization tools, and chatbots. It is also a key aspect of many job descriptions for AI Engineers, including those that mention Retrieval-Augmented Generation, agents, evaluations, fine-tuning, observability, and production concerns. For example, a job description might require the candidate to have experience with RAG models, or to be able to design and implement RAG systems for specific applications.
