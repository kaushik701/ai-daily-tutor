Day 14: Mastering Optimizers for AI Engineering Roles
TL;DR
This lesson covers the basics of optimizers, including SGD, Momentum, and Adam, to understand why Adam is often the default choice and when SGD might be a better option.

In plain English

Optimizers are algorithms used to adjust the model's parameters to minimize the loss function, which measures how well the model is doing.
The most basic optimizer is Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), which updates the parameters based on the gradient of the loss function. However, SGD can be slow to converge and may get stuck in local minima.
Momentum and Adam are two other popular optimizers that address these issues. Momentum adds a 'momentum' term to the update rule, which helps the model escape local minima, while Adam adapts the learning rate for each parameter based on the magnitude of the gradient.

Think of it this way

Think of an optimizer like a GPS navigation system. Just as a GPS helps you find the shortest route to your destination, an optimizer helps the model find the best set of parameters to minimize the loss function. Adam is like a GPS with real-time traffic updates, adjusting its route based on the current conditions, while SGD is like a GPS with outdated maps, which may not always find the best route.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

Understanding optimizers is crucial for AI Engineering roles, as it allows you to fine-tune models for specific tasks and evaluate their performance. For example, in a job description that mentions 'RAG' (Retrieve, Augment, Generate) models or 'evals' (evaluations), knowledge of optimizers can help you adjust the model's parameters to improve its performance. Additionally, understanding the trade-offs between different optimizers can help you address production concerns, such as model observability and Model Control Plane (MCP) integration.
One thing to remember
Adam is often the default optimizer due to its ability to adapt the learning rate and escape local minima, but SGD can be a better choice when the model is simple and the dataset is small.
