Day 19: Word Embeddings - From One-Hot to Dense Vectors
TL;DR
Word embeddings like Word2Vec and GloVe convert words into dense vectors, capturing semantic relationships between words

In plain English

Traditional methods of representing words, such as one-hot encoding, treat each word as a unique entity without considering its meaning or context. This approach can be limiting, especially when dealing with large vocabularies or trying to capture nuances in language.
Word embeddings, on the other hand, map words to dense vectors in a high-dimensional space, where semantically similar words are closer together. This allows for more efficient and effective processing of text data.
For example, the vector representation of "king" minus the vector representation of "man" plus the vector representation of "woman" results in a vector close to the representation of "queen", demonstrating the ability of word embeddings to capture complex relationships between words.

Think of it this way

The process of creating word embeddings can be thought of as creating a map of a city, where each word is a landmark. Just as a map can show the proximity of different landmarks to each other, word embeddings show the proximity of different words to each other in terms of their meaning.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

Understanding word embeddings is crucial for AI Engineering and ML Engineering roles, particularly those involving Natural Language Processing (NLP), as it provides the foundation for modern NLP techniques such as text classification, language modeling, and machine translation, and is often referenced in job descriptions as a key skill, alongside other concepts like RAG, agents, evals, fine-tuning, observability, and production concerns.
One thing to remember
Word embeddings like Word2Vec and GloVe enable the capture of semantic relationships between words, allowing for more efficient and effective processing of text data.
