Day 7: Stochastic vs Batch vs Mini-batch Gradient Descent
TL;DR
Stochastic, batch, and mini-batch gradient descent are three variants of gradient descent that differ in how they use the training data to update model parameters.

In plain English

Gradient descent is a method used to train machine learning models by minimizing the difference between the model's predictions and the actual outputs.
The three variants of gradient descent differ in how much of the training data they use to update the model parameters at each step. Stochastic gradient descent uses one data point at a time, batch gradient descent uses the entire dataset, and mini-batch gradient descent uses a small subset of the data.
This difference in data usage affects the tradeoffs between noise, speed, and memory usage during training.

Think of it this way

The difference between stochastic, batch, and mini-batch gradient descent can be thought of like cooking a meal. If you're using a recipe, you can either taste and adjust the seasoning after adding each ingredient (stochastic), after adding all the ingredients (batch), or after adding a few ingredients at a time (mini-batch). Each approach has its own advantages and disadvantages, such as the time it takes to taste and adjust, the risk of over-seasoning, and the amount of ingredients you can handle at once.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

Understanding the tradeoffs between stochastic, batch, and mini-batch gradient descent is crucial for AI Engineering roles, as it affects the model's performance, training time, and memory usage. For example, in a production environment, using mini-batch gradient descent can help balance the need for fast training and low memory usage, while also reducing the noise in the gradient updates, which is important for model reliability and observability, as mentioned in many AI Engineering job descriptions, such as those requiring experience with model fine-tuning and evaluation.
One thing to remember
The choice of gradient descent variant depends on the specific problem and dataset, and there is no one-size-fits-all solution.
