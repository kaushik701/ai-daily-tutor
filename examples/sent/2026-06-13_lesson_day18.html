Day 18: Understanding Recurrent Neural Networks and LSTMs
TL;DR
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTMs) networks are crucial for sequence modeling tasks, addressing the vanishing gradient problem that arises in traditional RNNs

In plain English

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are a type of neural network designed to handle sequential data, such as time series data or natural language processing. They do this by maintaining an internal state that captures information from previous inputs, allowing them to keep track of what has happened so far in the sequence.
RNNs, however, suffer from the vanishing gradient problem, which occurs when gradients are backpropagated through time, causing them to become very small, making it difficult to train the network. This issue led to the development of LSTMs, which are a special type of RNN designed to handle this problem by introducing memory cells that can learn to forget or remember information over long periods of time.
LSTMs are particularly useful for tasks that require understanding the context of a sequence, such as language translation or speech recognition, and their ability to handle long-term dependencies has made them a crucial component of many state-of-the-art models.

Think of it this way

Think of RNNs like a chef trying to recall a recipe they learned years ago. As they try to recall each step, they may forget some details or struggle to remember the order of ingredients. LSTMs are like a chef with a notebook, where they can write down important details and refer back to them as needed, making it easier to recall the entire recipe even after a long time.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

Understanding RNNs and LSTMs is crucial for AI Engineering roles, particularly those involving sequence modeling tasks, such as natural language processing or time series forecasting. The historical context of RNNs and the motivation behind the development of LSTMs can help engineers understand the importance of addressing issues like vanishing gradients, and how techniques like attention mechanisms were later developed to further improve sequence modeling capabilities, which are now essential skills highlighted in job descriptions, such as those requiring expertise in RAG, agents, evaluations, fine-tuning, observability, and production concerns.
One thing to remember
LSTMs were designed to address the vanishing gradient problem in traditional RNNs, allowing them to learn long-term dependencies in sequential data.
