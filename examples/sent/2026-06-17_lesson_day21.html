Day 21: Understanding Transformers and Self-Attention
TL;DR
Transformers have become a crucial component in many natural language processing tasks, replacing traditional recurrent neural networks (RNNs) due to their ability to handle sequential data more efficiently.

In plain English

Transformers are a type of neural network architecture that relies on self-attention mechanisms to process input sequences. This is different from RNNs, which process sequences one step at a time, using the previous steps to inform the current one.
The self-attention mechanism allows the model to attend to all positions in the input sequence simultaneously and weigh their importance. This is particularly useful for tasks that require understanding the relationships between different parts of the input sequence.
Multi-head attention is an extension of self-attention, where the model applies multiple attention mechanisms in parallel, allowing it to capture different types of relationships between the input elements.

Think of it this way

Imagine you're trying to understand a conversation between multiple people. A traditional RNN would be like listening to the conversation one person at a time, trying to remember what the previous person said to understand the current person's response. In contrast, a Transformer with self-attention would be like being able to hear all the people talking at the same time and focusing on the most relevant conversations to understand the context.

Why it matters for AI Eng interviews

Understanding Transformers and self-attention is crucial for AI Engineering roles, as it is a key component in many natural language processing tasks, such as language translation, sentiment analysis, and text summarization. Many job descriptions, such as those requiring expertise in RAG, agents, and fine-tuning, assume a strong foundation in Transformer architectures and their applications.
One thing to remember
Transformers replaced RNNs in many NLP tasks due to their ability to handle sequential data more efficiently and capture complex relationships between input elements using self-attention mechanisms.
